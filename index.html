How Do I Appeal?

If your violation was captured by automated traffic law enforcement such as a red light camera, you have 21 days from the date you are issued your Notice of Violation to contest the ticket. Otherwise, you have 7 days to contest the ticket; however, you will be sent a Notice of Violation if no response is recieved within 7 days and it functions the same as the Notice of Violation for automated traffic enforcement. If you do not reply to the Notice of Violation within 21 days, you will get a Notice of Determination which will give you another 21 days to contest the ticket. After the 21 days of the Notice of Determination expire, you will recieve a Notice of Final Determination and will have forfeited all rights to challenge your ticket.

There are many ways to contest your ticket, but we strongly feel that the best method is to contest in-person. Rhetorical Appeals

Logos

Logos is an appeal to the audience's reason. When you make your appeal, you want to primarily rely on the logical appeal. You will want to explain to the judge clearly and with evidence how you did not actually commit the violation of which you were accused and cite the specific allowed defense that you are using to make your case.

Pathos

Pathos is an appeal to the audience's emotions. This is the appeal that you avoid. The judge does not want to hear about how your sister is sick in the hospital because he's heard it all a million times before.

Ethos

Ethos is an appeal to authority. Again, you should not very heavily rely on this rhetorical appeal when you are challenging your ticket. However, do ensure that you are addressing the judge with his proper title such as calling them "sir/ma'am" or "your honor"

FAQ

Q: Should I appeal my ticket even if I am guilty of the violation?

A: Absoulutely. For instance if you are ticketed for not having an expired city sticker or license plate, if you correct the issue before your hearing or if your violation occurred between 04/01/2022-12/31/2023 you are not liable. Motorists that recieve parking tickets, such as the ticket expert mentioned before, have won hearings by showing the judge pictures of faded or obscured signs in the zone in which they illegally parked.

Q: What if I am unable to make it to court?

A: You can still appeal your ticket by mail, online correspondence, or via a virtual hearing. To appeal by mail you will need to include the stub at the end of your Notice of Violation and fill in the bubble to contest by mail. If appealing via online correspondence, you can use the same form as you would to contest in-person, but just select the option for correspondence or for a virtual hearing. However if possible, it is better if you show up to your trial live.
